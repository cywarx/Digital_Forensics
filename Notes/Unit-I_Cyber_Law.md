## 1. The World Wide Web (WWW)

> [!abstract] Definition
> The **World Wide Web (WWW)** is a system of interlinked hypertext documents and multimedia content accessed via the **Internet** using a web browser. It was invented by **Sir Tim Berners-Lee** in **1989** at CERN.

### 🔑 Key Concepts

| Term           | Meaning                                              |
| -------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **URL**        | Uniform Resource Locator — address of a web resource |
| **HTTP/HTTPS** | Protocol used to transfer web pages                  |
| **HTML**       | Language used to build web pages                     |
| **Browser**    | Software to access the Web (Chrome, Firefox)         |
| **Hyperlink**  | Clickable connection between web pages               |
| **Web Server** | Stores and serves web content                        |

### 📌 WWW vs Internet

| Feature    | Internet                        | WWW                               |
| ---------- | ------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| What it is | Physical network infrastructure | A service running on the Internet |
| Invented   | 1960s (ARPANET)                 | 1989 (Tim Berners-Lee)            |
| Examples   | Email, FTP, VoIP                | Websites, web apps                |

> [!tip] Exam Tip
> The **Internet** is the infrastructure (hardware + protocols); the **Web** is one service *on top of* the Internet.

## 2. Web-Centric Business

> [!abstract] Definition
> A **Web-Centric Business** is one that places the website and the web experience at the **core of its operations**, marketing, customer service, and revenue generation.

### 🔑 Characteristics

- Primary customer interface is **online**
- Business processes are **integrated with the web**
- Uses the Internet for **marketing, sales, delivery, and support**
- Examples: Amazon, Flipkart, Google

### 🔄 Evolution

```
Traditional Business → E-enabled Business → Web-Centric Business → Digital-Native Business
```

### Benefits & Challenges

> [!success] Benefits
> - 24/7 availability
> - Global reach
> - Lower operational costs
> - Data-driven customer insights

> [!warning] Challenges
> - Cybersecurity threats
> - Digital divide
> - Privacy concerns
> - Dependency on technology uptime

## 3. E-Business Architecture

> [!abstract] Definition
> **E-Business Architecture** refers to the framework of technologies, systems, processes, and people that together enable an organization to conduct business electronically.

### 🏗️ Layers of E-Business Architecture

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────┐
│        Presentation Layer              │  ← Web Browser / Mobile App
├────────────────────────────────────────┤
│         Business Logic Layer           │  ← Application Servers
├────────────────────────────────────────┤
│           Data Layer                   │  ← Databases (MySQL, Oracle)
├────────────────────────────────────────┤
│        Integration Layer               │  ← APIs, Middleware, ERP
├────────────────────────────────────────┤
│        Infrastructure Layer            │  ← Servers, Cloud, Network
└────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 🔑 Core Components

| Component | Role |
|---|---|
| **Web Server** | Handles HTTP requests |
| **Application Server** | Executes business logic |
| **Database Server** | Stores and retrieves data |
| **Payment Gateway** | Processes financial transactions |
| **Firewall / Security** | Protects the architecture |
| **CDN** | Delivers content quickly worldwide |

> [!note] Key Technologies
> - **Front-end:** HTML, CSS, JavaScript, React
> - **Back-end:** PHP, Node.js, Python, Java
> - **Database:** MySQL, MongoDB, PostgreSQL
> - **Cloud:** AWS, Azure, GCP

## 4. Models of E-Business

> [!abstract] Overview
> E-Business models define how a business creates, delivers, and captures value through electronic means.

### 📊 Primary E-Business Models

| Model | Full Form | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|---|

- **Advertising Model** — revenue from ads (Google)
- **Transaction Fee Model** — cut per transaction (PayPal)

- **Affiliate Model** — commission for referrals

## 5. E-Commerce

> [!abstract] Definition
> **E-Commerce (Electronic Commerce)** refers to buying and selling of goods and services over the **Internet**, along with the associated data transfer and financial transactions.

### 🔑 Types of E-Commerce

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Direct Selling** | Manufacturer sells directly online |
| **Online Marketplace** | Platform hosts multiple sellers (Amazon) |
| **Drop Shipping** | Retailer sells without holding stock |
| **Digital Products** | Software, music, e-books sold online |
| **Subscription Commerce** | Recurring delivery of products/services |

### E-Commerce vs E-Business

| Feature | E-Commerce | E-Business |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | Narrower (only buying/selling) | Broader (all electronic business activities) |
| Focus | Transactions | Entire business operations |
| Includes | E-Business? | ❌ No | ✅ Yes |

### 🔐 Security in E-Commerce

> [!warning] Key Threats
> - **Phishing** — fake sites to steal credentials
> - **Payment fraud** — unauthorized card usage
> - **Man-in-the-Middle** — intercepting transactions
> - **SQL Injection** — database attacks on e-commerce backend

> [!tip] Protective Measures
> - SSL/TLS encryption (HTTPS)
> - Two-factor authentication (2FA)
> - PCI-DSS compliance for payment systems
> - Secure coding practices

## 6. Threats to the Virtual World

> [!abstract] Overview
> The **virtual world** encompasses cyberspace — all digital environments, networks, systems, and data. Threats to the virtual world are diverse and constantly evolving.

### 🛡️ Classification of Cyber Threats

#### Technical Threats
| Threat | Description |
|---|---|
| **Malware** | Viruses, Trojans, Ransomware, Worms, Spyware |
| **DDoS Attack** | Overwhelming a server with traffic |
| **Zero-Day Exploit** | Attacks on unknown/unpatched vulnerabilities |
| **SQL Injection** | Injecting malicious SQL to manipulate databases |
| **XSS** | Cross-Site Scripting — injecting client-side scripts |
| **MITM** | Man-in-the-Middle — intercepting communications |

#### Social Engineering Threats
| Threat | Description |
|---|---|
| **Phishing** | Deceptive emails/links to steal credentials |
| **Spear Phishing** | Targeted phishing on specific individuals |
| **Vishing** | Voice-based phishing |
| **Smishing** | SMS-based phishing |
| **Pretexting** | Fabricated scenarios to extract information |

#### Legal/Social Threats
- Identity Theft
- Cyber Stalking
- Online Harassment
- Fake News / Misinformation

> [!danger] Critical Insight
> Threats in the virtual world are **borderless** — an attacker in one country can target systems in another, making jurisdiction and enforcement complex under cyber law.

## 7. Cybercrimes & Social Media

> [!abstract] Definition
> **Cybercrime** is any criminal activity involving a computer, network, or the internet — either as the tool, target, or both.

### 📋 Categories of Cybercrime

#### Against Individuals
- Cyberstalking
- Online harassment / bullying
- Identity theft
- Cyber defamation
- Morphing (editing someone's photo maliciously)

#### Against Organizations
- Hacking / Unauthorized access
- Data theft / Corporate espionage
- Ransomware attacks
- Website defacement

#### Against the State/Nation
- Cyber terrorism
- Cyber warfare
- Critical infrastructure attacks

### 📱 Cybercrimes on Social Media

> [!example] Common Social Media Crimes
> | Crime | Description |
> |---|---|
> | **Fake Profiles** | Creating profiles impersonating real people |
> | **Cyberbullying** | Harassment, threats, humiliation online |
> | **Revenge Porn** | Non-consensual sharing of intimate images |
> | **Morphing** | Digitally altering images to defame |
> | **Doxxing** | Publicly revealing private information |
> | **Clickbait Scams** | Malicious links disguised as viral content |
> | **Account Hacking** | Unauthorized access to social accounts |

### ⚖️ Legal Provisions (IT Act 2000)

| Section | Offence |
|---|---|
| Sec. 66 | Hacking / Computer fraud |
| Sec. 66A (struck down) | Offensive messages (Shreya Singhal v. Union of India, 2015) |
| Sec. 66C | Identity theft |
| Sec. 66D | Cheating by impersonation |
| Sec. 66E | Violation of privacy |
| Sec. 67 | Publishing obscene material online |
| Sec. 67A | Publishing sexually explicit content |
| Sec. 67B | Child pornography |

> [!warning] Important Case
> **Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015)** — SC struck down **Section 66A** of IT Act as unconstitutional for violating freedom of speech under Article 19(1)(a).

## 8. Cyber Squatting

> [!abstract] Definition
> **Cyber Squatting** (also called **Domain Squatting**) is the practice of **registering, trafficking in, or using a domain name** with bad faith intent to profit from the goodwill of a **trademark** belonging to someone else.

### 🔑 How It Works

```
Victim has trademark "BrandX"
       ↓
Squatter registers "brandx.com" before BrandX does
       ↓
Squatter demands money to transfer the domain
       OR uses it to divert BrandX's traffic
```

### 📌 Types of Cyber Squatting

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Classic Squatting** | Registering a brand name domain to sell later |
| **Typosquatting** | Registering typo versions (gooogle.com) |
| **Reverse Cyber Squatting** | Brand trying to take legitimate domain owner's domain |
| **Name Jacking** | Registering celebrity names as domains |

### ⚖️ Legal Remedies

- **ICANN's UDRP** (Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy)
- **Indian Trademark Act, 1999** — protects brand against domain misuse
- **IT Act, 2000** — Sec. 43 (unauthorized use)
- Civil suit for passing off / trademark infringement

> [!example] Famous Cases
> - **Yahoo! Inc. v. Akash Arora (1999)** — Delhi HC: "yahooindia.com" was cyber squatting
> - **Rediff Communication v. Cyberbooth (1999)** — "redif.com" held as cyber squatting

## 9. Cyber Espionage

> [!abstract] Definition
> **Cyber Espionage** is the use of cyber techniques to **secretly access, steal, or monitor classified, sensitive, or proprietary information** from governments, organizations, or individuals for political, military, or economic advantage.

### 🔑 Key Features

- Usually **state-sponsored** or by **organised groups**
- Target: Government secrets, military data, IP, trade secrets
- Often goes **undetected for months or years** (APTs)
- Uses sophisticated tools — RATs, keyloggers, zero-days

### 🕵️ Methods Used

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **Spear Phishing** | Targeted email attacks on specific officials |
| **Watering Hole** | Infecting websites frequented by targets |
| **Supply Chain Attack** | Compromising vendor software/hardware |
| **APT (Advanced Persistent Threat)** | Long-term covert infiltration |
| **Zero-Day Exploits** | Using unknown vulnerabilities |
| **Insider Threats** | Recruiting insiders to steal data |

### 🌍 Notable Examples

> [!example] Real-World Incidents
> - **Stuxnet (2010)** — US/Israel cyber operation against Iran's nuclear centrifuges
> - **Operation Aurora (2009)** — China-attributed attacks on Google, Adobe
> - **Shadow Network** — Cyber espionage campaign targeting Indian govt. systems
> - **SolarWinds (2020)** — Massive supply chain espionage attributed to Russia

### ⚖️ Indian Legal Framework

- **IT Act, 2000 — Sec. 66** (Hacking)
- **Official Secrets Act, 1923** — espionage of government information
- **National Cyber Security Policy, 2013**

## 10. Cyber Warfare

> [!abstract] Definition
> **Cyber Warfare** refers to **state-sponsored or nation-level attacks** on another country's computer systems, critical infrastructure, or information systems as an act of war or as a complement to traditional warfare.

### ⚔️ Targets of Cyber Warfare

```
Power Grids → Financial Systems → Military Networks
     ↓               ↓                  ↓
Communication → Water Treatment → Defense Systems
```

### 📌 Types of Cyber Warfare Attacks

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sabotage** | Destroying or disrupting enemy infrastructure |
| **Espionage** | Stealing military/government secrets |
| **DDoS** | Overwhelming government/military websites |
| **Propaganda** | Spreading misinformation to destabilize |
| **Ransomware** | Encrypting critical govt. systems |

### 🌍 Documented Examples

> [!example]
> - **Estonia (2007)** — Russia-attributed massive DDoS attacks on banks, media, government
> - **Georgia (2008)** — Cyber attacks alongside physical military invasion
> - **Ukraine Power Grid (2015-16)** — Russian attacks causing blackouts
> - **NotPetya (2017)** — Destructive malware targeting Ukraine, spread globally

### ⚖️ Legal Status

> [!note] International Law Gap
> - No dedicated international treaty on cyber warfare
> - **Tallinn Manual** — NATO-commissioned guide applying existing international law to cyber conflicts
> - **UN GGE (Group of Governmental Experts)** — developing norms for responsible state behaviour in cyberspace

## 11. Cyber Terrorism

> [!abstract] Definition
> **Cyber Terrorism** refers to the **intentional use of computers, networks, and the Internet to cause severe disruption**, fear, or harm to critical infrastructure or government systems for **political, ideological, or religious goals**.

### 🔑 Distinction: Cyber Terrorism vs Cyber Warfare

| Feature | Cyber Terrorism | Cyber Warfare |
|---|---|---|
| Actor | Non-state actors (terrorists) | Nation-states |
| Motive | Ideology, religion, politics | Strategic national interests |
| Target | Civilians, public infrastructure | Military, government |
| Scale | Selective attacks | Large-scale, coordinated |

### 🎯 Potential Targets

- Nuclear facilities
- Power and water supply systems
- Stock exchanges and banking
- Air traffic control systems
- Emergency services (police, hospitals)

### 📌 Methods

- Malware deployment on critical infrastructure
- Website defacement with propaganda
- Coordinated DDoS on government portals
- Social media use for recruitment and radicalization

### ⚖️ Indian Legal Framework

> [!important] IT Act — Section 66F
> **Cyber Terrorism** is defined and punishable under **Section 66F of the IT Act, 2000** (inserted by the IT Amendment Act, 2008).
>
> Punishment: **Imprisonment for life**
>
> Includes: unauthorized access to critical infrastructure, introducing malware, disrupting essential services

- **Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), 1967** — covers terrorism including cyber means
- **National Cyber Security Policy, 2013** — framework to combat cyber terrorism

## 12. Cyber Defamation

> [!abstract] Definition
> **Cyber Defamation** is the act of making **false and damaging statements** about a person or organization through **online platforms** — websites, social media, emails, blogs, or any digital medium — which harms their reputation.

### 📌 Elements of Defamation (Online or Offline)

```
1. The statement must be FALSE
2. It must be PUBLISHED (communicated to a third party)
3. It must IDENTIFY the victim
4. It must cause HARM to reputation
```

### 🔑 Types

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Libel** | Written/published defamation (most cyber defamation is libel) |
| **Slander** | Spoken defamation (rare in cyber context) |

### 🔍 Forms of Cyber Defamation

- Posting false news on websites/social media
- Sending defamatory emails
- Creating fake profiles making false claims
- Posting doctored images/videos (morphing)
- Negative reviews with false claims

### ⚖️ Legal Provisions

| Law | Section | Provision |
|---|---|---|
| **Indian Penal Code (IPC)** | Sec. 499, 500 | Definition and punishment for defamation |
| **IT Act, 2000** | Sec. 66A (struck down) | Was used for cyber defamation |
| **IT Act, 2000** | Sec. 67 | Publishing obscene/defamatory content |
| **IT Act, 2000** | Sec. 66E | Violation of privacy (overlapping) |

> [!warning] Post Shreya Singhal (2015)
> After Sec. 66A was struck down, **IPC Sec. 499/500** remains the primary provision for cyber defamation. Civil suits for damages are also available.

> [!example] Case Law
> **SMC Pneumatics v. Jogesh Kwatra (2014)** — Delhi HC granted India's **first injunction** in a cyber defamation case, restraining an employee from sending defamatory emails about the employer.

## 14. Misuse of Individual Information

> [!abstract] Definition
> Misuse of individual information refers to the **unauthorized collection, use, disclosure, alteration, or destruction** of personal data causing harm to the individual.

### 📌 Forms of Misuse

| Form | Description |
|---|---|
| **Identity Theft** | Using someone's identity for fraud |
| **Data Harvesting** | Collecting personal data without consent |
| **Profiling** | Building personal profiles from online behaviour |
| **Doxxing** | Publicly releasing private information |
| **Financial Fraud** | Using banking/card details without authorization |
| **Medical Data Misuse** | Leaking health records for discrimination |
| **Phishing** | Tricking users into giving personal data |

### 🔑 Key Concept: Personal Data vs Sensitive Personal Data

> [!info] IT (Reasonable Security Practices) Rules, 2011
> **Sensitive Personal Data or Information (SPDI)** includes:
> - Passwords
> - Financial information (bank accounts, credit cards)
> - Health/medical records
> - Sexual orientation
> - Biometric data

### ⚖️ Legal Framework

| Law | Provision |
|---|---|
| **IT Act, 2000 — Sec. 43A** | Compensation for failure to protect sensitive personal data by body corporates |
| **IT Act, 2000 — Sec. 72A** | Punishment for disclosure of information in breach of lawful contract |
| **IT (Amendment) Act, 2008** | Enhanced penalties for data privacy violations |
| **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDPA)** | India's dedicated data protection law |

> [!important] DPDPA 2023
> The **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023** is India's landmark data protection legislation. Key features:
> - **Data Principal** = Individual whose data is collected
> - **Data Fiduciary** = Organization that processes data
> - **Consent** required for data processing
> - **Data Localisation** provisions
> - **Penalties** up to ₹250 crore

## 15. Information Technology Act, 2000

> [!abstract] Overview
> The **Information Technology Act, 2000** is India's primary legislation governing **cyberspace, electronic transactions, digital signatures, and cybercrimes**. It was enacted on **17 October 2000** and is based on the **UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce, 1996**.

### 📜 Historical Background

```
UNCITRAL Model Law (1996)
        ↓
IT Bill introduced in Parliament (1999)
        ↓
IT Act, 2000 passed — enacted 17 Oct 2000
        ↓
IT (Amendment) Act, 2008 — major amendments
        ↓
IT Rules (2011) — Intermediary Guidelines, SPDI Rules
        ↓
IT (Intermediary Guidelines & Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules, 2021
```

---

### 🎯 Objectives of the IT Act, 2000

> [!note] Key Objectives (Preamble)
> 1. **Legal recognition** of electronic records and digital signatures
> 2. **Facilitate** e-commerce and e-governance
> 3. **Prevent** computer crimes and misuse
> 4. **Amend** Indian Penal Code, Indian Evidence Act, Bankers' Books Evidence Act, Reserve Bank of India Act
> 5. **Enable** digital contracts and transactions

---

### 📌 Applicability of the IT Act, 2000

> [!info] Section 1(2) — Applicability
> The Act **applies to the whole of India** and also applies to offences **committed outside India** if the computer used is in India (extraterritorial jurisdiction — Section 75).

#### ✅ Where IT Act Applies

- Electronic contracts and transactions
- Digital signatures and electronic records
- Cybercrimes committed online
- Data protection and privacy
- Intermediary liability

---

### ❌ Non-Applicability (Exceptions)

> [!important] Section 1(4) — Non-Applicability
> The IT Act does **NOT apply** to:
>
> 1. **Negotiable Instruments** (except cheques) under the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881
> 2. **Power of Attorney** under the Powers-of-Attorney Act, 1882
> 3. **Trust Deeds** under the Indian Trusts Act, 1882
> 4. **Wills** under the Indian Succession Act, 1925
> 5. **Contracts for sale/conveyance of immovable property** under the Transfer of Property Act, 1882
> 6. **Any class of documents notified by the Central Government**

> [!tip] Memory Aid — NPTWISC
> **N**egotiable instruments | **P**ower of attorney | **T**rust deeds | **W**ills | **I**mmovable property contracts | **S**pecified by Central Govt. | **C**heques (applicable)

### 🏛️ Adjudicatory Framework

```
Cyber Crime committed
        ↓
Complaint filed at Police / Cybercrime Cell
        ↓
Investigation by designated Cyber Police Station
        ↓
Adjudicating Officer (Sec. 46) — civil contraventions
    OR
Court of Session — criminal offences
        ↓
Appeal: Cyber Appellate Tribunal (CyAT) — Sec. 48
        ↓
High Court → Supreme Court
```

> [!info] Designated Cyber Courts
> Under the IT Act, cases involving imprisonment exceeding 3 years are tried by the **Court of Session**. The **Cyber Appellate Tribunal** hears appeals against Adjudicating Officers' decisions.
